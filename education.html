Education

Learning Begins with Curiosity

HoneyTide Apiary is committed to helping people better understand honey bees, native pollinators, swarms, sustainable beekeeping, and the living systems that support our food, gardens, and communities.

Why Education Matters

Bees Are Part of a Larger Story

Honey bees are only one part of Oregon's pollinator landscape. Native bees, flowering plants, gardens, farms, forests, and managed colonies are all connected through the work of pollination.

This page is designed for homeowners, curious learners, future beekeepers, gardeners, and anyone who wants to support pollinators in practical ways.

Native pollinators play a vital role in Oregon ecosystems. While HoneyTide primarily works with honey bees, healthy landscapes support a diversity of pollinators including bumble bees, mason bees, leafcutter bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, flies, and other beneficial insects.

Honey Bees & Native Bees

Honey bees live in large managed colonies, while Oregon is also home to hundreds of native bee species with different nesting habits, plant relationships, and seasonal patterns.

Supporting pollinators means caring about more than one species. Healthy landscapes need diverse bees, diverse flowers, and reduced pesticide pressure.

What Is a Swarm?

A swarm is a natural part of honey bee reproduction. When a colony grows strong enough, a queen and a group of workers may leave to search for a new home.

Swarms are often temporary and may cluster on tree branches, fences, shrubs, or outdoor structures while scout bees search for a permanent location.

When HoneyTide Can Help

HoneyTide may be able to assist with easy-access honey bee swarms in the Springfield/Eugene and surrounding areas, especially swarms hanging from trees, branches, fences, shrubs, or open outdoor spaces.

HoneyTide does not currently offer structural removals that require opening walls, roofs, buildings, or taking apart major property structures.

Future Beginner Classes

As HoneyTide grows, future educational offerings may include beginner beekeeping classes, pollinator awareness sessions, swarm education, and hands-on learning opportunities for people interested in sustainable beekeeping.

These future classes will be rooted in curiosity, safety, stewardship, and respect for both bees and the natural world.

Found a Swarm?

Stay Calm, Keep Distance, and Ask for Help

If you find a honey bee swarm, avoid spraying it, shaking it, or disturbing it. Keep people and pets back, take a clear photo if safe, and contact a local beekeeper or swarm resource.

Helpful details include the swarm's location, height from the ground, size, how long it has been there, and whether it is easy to access.

Pollinator Support

Simple Ways to Help Pollinators

Plant for Bloom Succession

Choose flowers that bloom across spring, summer, and fall so pollinators have food throughout the season.

Reduce Pesticide Use

Avoid spraying blooming plants and consider lower-impact pest management practices whenever possible.

Support Native Habitat

Native plants, undisturbed soil, hollow stems, and natural edges can support many native bee species.
